FLIGHT CONCEPTS

Understanding the physics and forces that make rockets fly

1

Thrust

Thrust is the force that pushes a rocket upward and forward. It's created when the rocket engine burns fuel and expels hot gases at high speed out of the nozzle.

How thrust is generated: expelled mass creates an equal and opposite reaction force

Think of it like this: When you blow up a balloon and let it go, the air rushing out pushes the balloon forward. Rocket thrust works the same way, but much more powerfully!

3

Air Resistance (Drag)

Air resistance, also called drag, is the force that opposes a rocket's motion through the air. As the rocket moves upward, it has to push air molecules out of the way, which slows it down.

Factors That Increase Drag:

  • Higher speed
  • Larger surface area
  • Rough or irregular surfaces
  • Blunt nose cone shapes

How to Reduce Drag:

  • Use a pointed nose cone
  • Keep the rocket smooth and streamlined
  • Minimise surface area facing the airflow
  • Ensure fins are properly aligned
4

Centre of Gravity (CG)

The centre of gravity (CG) is the point where all of the rocket's weight is balanced. If you could balance the rocket on your finger, this is where you'd place it.

Finding the CG

You can find the centre of gravity by balancing your rocket horizontally on your finger or the edge of a ruler. The point where it balances is the CG.

Important: The CG changes as fuel burns! A rocket's CG moves forward as fuel is used up.

The CG is usually located in the lower half of the rocket, where most of the mass (engine and fuel) is concentrated.

5

Centre of Pressure (CP)

The centre of pressure (CP) is the point where all the aerodynamic forces (like drag and lift) act on the rocket. It's determined by the rocket's shape, especially the fins and nose cone.

The Golden Rule of Rocket Stability

The CP must be BEHIND (below) the CG for stable flight!

Think of it like this: Imagine throwing a dart. The heavy tip (CG) is at the front, and the feathers (which create the CP) are at the back. This keeps the dart flying straight!

6

Recovery System

The recovery system is what brings your rocket safely back to the ground after launch. Without it, the rocket would crash and be destroyed!

Parachute

The most common recovery system. Deploys at apogee and slows the rocket's descent.

Streamer

A long ribbon that creates drag. Used for lighter rockets or when you want faster descent.

Helicopter Recovery

Fins spin like helicopter blades, providing a controlled, slow descent.

Key Components

  • Ejection charge: Small explosive that pushes the nose cone off
  • Shock cord: Strong elastic cord that connects the parts
  • Parachute/streamer: Creates drag to slow descent
  • Wadding: Fireproof material that protects the parachute from hot gases
7

Thrust Curve

A thrust curve is a graph that shows how much thrust a rocket engine produces over time. It helps engineers and rocketeers understand how the engine performs throughout the burn.

Understanding the Curve

The thrust curve typically has three main phases:

  • Initial spike: Thrust jumps quickly as the engine ignites
  • Sustained burn: Thrust remains relatively constant as fuel burns
  • Tail-off: Thrust drops as fuel runs out

What It Tells Us

  • Total impulse: Area under the curve (measured in Newton-seconds)
  • Average thrust: Typical force during burn
  • Peak thrust: Maximum force produced
  • Burn time: How long the engine fires

Why Thrust Curves Matter

Thrust curves help you choose the right motor for your rocket. A high-thrust, short-burn motor gives quick acceleration (good for heavy rockets), while a low-thrust, long-burn motor provides sustained, gentler acceleration (good for altitude records).

8

Apogee

Apogee is the highest point in a rocket's flight. At apogee, the rocket momentarily stops climbing before it begins to fall back to Earth. This is the critical moment when the recovery system typically deploys.

What Happens at Apogee

  • Upward velocity reaches zero: The rocket stops climbing
  • Gravitational force takes over: Rocket begins to fall
  • Recovery deployment: Parachute or streamer deploys
  • Maximum altitude recorded: Altimeters log peak height

Predicting Apogee

Apogee can be calculated using factors like:

  • Total impulse of the motor
  • Mass of the rocket
  • Drag coefficient
  • Cross-sectional area

Competition rocketeers aim for specific target altitudes by carefully calculating and testing their designs to reach apogee at exactly the right height!

ROCKET PARTS

The physical components that make up a rocket

1

Nose Cone

The nose cone is the pointed tip at the front of the rocket. It's the first part to push through the air, so its shape has a huge impact on how efficiently the rocket flies.

Purpose of the Nose Cone

  • Reduces drag: A pointed shape cuts through the air more easily than a flat surface
  • Protects payload: Houses sensitive equipment like altimeters
  • Improves stability: Helps keep the rocket pointed in the right direction
  • Deploys recovery: Often contains the parachute and ejection mechanism

Design choice: Different nose cone shapes have different aerodynamic properties. More pointed shapes reduce drag but may be harder to manufacture!

Body Tube

The body tube is the main cylindrical section of the rocket. It holds everything together and provides the structural framework for all other components.

Functions

  • Structure: Holds all rocket parts in place
  • Housing: Contains the motor, parachute, and payload
  • Aerodynamics: Provides a smooth surface for airflow
  • Attachment point: Where fins are mounted

Materials

  • Cardboard: Light and cheap (beginner rockets)
  • Plastic: Durable and weather-resistant
  • Fibreglass: Strong and lightweight (high-performance)
  • Carbon fibre: Strongest and lightest (professional)

Important Measurements

Diameter

Width of the tube (e.g., 24mm, 38mm)

Length

Height of the rocket

Wall Thickness

Affects strength and weight

5

Altimeter

An altimeter is an electronic device that measures how high a rocket flies. It works by detecting changes in air pressure, which decreases as altitude increases.

How It Works

  • Measures air pressure continuously during flight
  • Converts pressure readings to altitude
  • Records maximum altitude reached
  • Some can detect when the rocket is descending

Data Recorded

  • Maximum altitude
  • Flight time
  • Speed and acceleration
  • Time to apogee (highest point)

6

Fins

Fins are the flat surfaces attached to the bottom of the rocket. They're one of the most important parts for keeping your rocket flying straight!

How Fins Work

Fins create the centre of pressure (CP) that keeps your rocket stable. They work like the feathers on an arrow:

  • If the rocket tilts, the fins catch more air on one side
  • This creates a correcting force that straightens the rocket
  • Larger fins move the CP further back, increasing stability
  • Most rockets have 3 or 4 fins for balanced control

Good Fin Design

  • Rigid and firmly attached
  • Symmetrically placed
  • Large enough for stability
  • Smooth, streamlined edges

Poor Fin Design

  • Wobbly or loose fins
  • Uneven or misaligned
  • Too small for the rocket
  • Rough or damaged edges
7

Fin Shapes

Different fin shapes offer different advantages. Drag on 3D models to rotate and examine them!

Trapezoidal

A classic fin shape that looks like a trapezoid (four-sided shape with one pair of parallel sides). This is the most common fin design in model rocketry.

Advantages:

  • Easy to design and cut
  • Good stability
  • Efficient use of material
  • Strong structural design

Disadvantages:

  • Sharp corners can create small vortices
  • Slightly more drag than swept shapes

Best for: General-purpose rockets, beginners, and most model rocket applications

Elliptical

A fin with smooth, curved edges that form an ellipse or oval shape. This aerodynamic design is often used in high-performance rockets.

Advantages:

  • Lowest drag of any fin shape
  • Smooth airflow around edges
  • Excellent high-speed performance
  • Reduces turbulence

Disadvantages:

  • Harder to cut accurately
  • Requires more material
  • More difficult to attach to body tube
  • May be weaker at the tips

Best for: High-performance rockets, speed records, and when drag reduction is critical

Tube Fins

Tube fins are hollow cylindrical tubes mounted at the rear of the rocket, perpendicular to the body. They provide an alternative to traditional flat fins and can reduce drag while maintaining stability.

Advantages:

  • Lower drag than flat fins at high speeds
  • Can hold components (cameras, electronics)
  • Strong structural design
  • Creates smooth airflow
  • Unique, modern appearance

Disadvantages:

  • More difficult to manufacture
  • Heavier than flat fins
  • Harder to attach to body tube
  • More complex design calculations

Best for: High-performance rockets, advanced builders, and designs where internal space for electronics is valuable
